How much do you actually need invested to live off dividends?

Updated April 23, 2026 · Written by the YieldOS team

"Live off dividends" is the holy grail of passive-income investing — and also where most people drastically underestimate how big their portfolio needs to be. The math is simple, but the assumptions underneath it matter a lot. This post walks through the real numbers for 2026, shows you the formula, and lets you plug in your own life so you know exactly where your finish line sits.

Short answer: At a realistic 4% average yield, you need roughly 300× your monthly expenses invested to live on dividends alone. Spending $4,000/month? You need about $1.2M. The two big levers that change this number are your average yield and whether you reinvest growth.

The one formula that runs this entire post

Everything else is just commentary around this equation:

Portfolio needed = (Monthly expenses × 12) ÷ Average yield

If your annual expenses are $48,000 and your average yield is 4%, you need $1,200,000. That's it — that's the whole thing. The rest is figuring out what yield is actually safe, and what expenses you should plan for.

The yield question: how realistic is 4%?

Every yield assumption is a bet. Higher yields let you retire sooner but usually come with one of three costs: lower dividend growth, higher principal risk, or both. Here's how to think about the realistic range:

For a portfolio you actually want to live on, 3.5–5% is the honest, sustainable range. You can push to 6–7% with covered-call ETFs if you accept that your payout won't grow much, which means inflation will eat into it over 20+ years.

The reinvestment shortcut (this is where the magic is)

Here's the part most articles skip: you don't need to hit your final number with fresh contributions. If you let dividends reinvest while you keep adding money, you finish way faster than the static math suggests.

Take someone investing $1,500/month into a portfolio averaging 4% yield + 8% dividend growth (a roughly SCHD-flavored blend). Total return is ~12% annualized. Starting from zero, they hit the $1.2M / $4,000-per-month target in about 21 years. Without any dividend growth or reinvestment, the same monthly contribution would take over 40 years. Reinvestment isn't a nice-to-have — it's most of the answer.

The real playbook: Target a 4% blended yield with dividend-growth exposure (SCHD, DGRO, aristocrats), reinvest everything until your monthly income crosses your expenses, then flip the DRIP off and start living on the cashflow.

What about inflation?

Inflation is the reason "300× your expenses" isn't quite enough over a 30+ year retirement. If you want your income to keep up with rising costs, your dividends need to grow too — which means you need dividend-growth stocks, not just high-yielders. A portfolio averaging 4% yield + 6% dividend growth will double its cashflow roughly every 12 years, easily beating historical inflation.

This is why most "I retired on JEPI" posts are dangerous. JEPI pays ~7.5% today, but the distribution hasn't grown meaningfully since inception. Ten years in, inflation will have cut its purchasing power by 25–30%. Unless you're over 70, you probably want a growth component.

Country-specific notes

US investors. Qualified dividends are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. Most dividend ETFs and individual large-caps pay qualified dividends. REITs and most covered-call ETFs pay ordinary-income dividends, which can hit your marginal rate. Plan accordingly — in a taxable account, a 4% qualified-dividend ETF beats a 5.5% REIT for most investors.

Canadian investors. Eligible Canadian dividends get the dividend tax credit, which dramatically lowers your effective rate (often to zero in the lowest bracket). US dividends in a non-registered account get hit with 15% US withholding plus Canadian tax. Hold US dividend stocks inside an RRSP to dodge the withholding. YieldOS supports TSX tickers natively.

UK investors. First £500 of dividends is tax-free; above that, you pay 8.75%, 33.75%, or 39.35% depending on your tax band. ISAs are the cheat code — dividends inside an ISA are completely tax-free forever.

Frequently asked questions

Can I live off $1M in dividends?

At a 4% average yield, $1M generates about $3,333/month pre-tax. That's enough to live on in most of the Midwest, the South, and much of rural America — but tight in coastal cities. Push the yield to 5.5% (via a higher REIT/covered-call allocation) and you get $4,583/month from the same $1M.

What's the safest yield to target?

3.5% to 4.5% from a diversified dividend-growth portfolio (SCHD + DGRO + blue chips) is the sweet spot. Dividend cuts are rare at this yield band, and payouts typically grow 7–9% annually.

Should I go all-in on high-yield ETFs like JEPI?

Only if you're near retirement. JEPI pays big today but historically doesn't grow its distribution. In a 30-year retirement, you'd watch inflation eat 50% of your purchasing power. Younger investors benefit from dividend growth over nominal yield.

Is the 4% rule the same as living off dividends?

No. The 4% rule assumes you're selling 4% of your portfolio annually. Living off dividends means your yield is your withdrawal — you never touch principal. The dividend approach is more portfolio-size-dependent but doesn't require timing the market.
