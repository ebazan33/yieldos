The 12 Best Monthly Dividend Stocks and ETFs for 2026

Updated April 23, 2026 · Ranked by income stability, not headline yield

Almost every investor starts here: "If I could just get paid every month instead of every quarter, my dividend portfolio would feel like a paycheck." This is that list — but ranked by how likely each dividend is to still be there in 2030, not by whatever number Seeking Alpha was excited about last week.

How we ranked them

We score every name on three dimensions, not just yield:

Income stability — How long has the dividend been paid without a cut? A 30-year streak (Realty Income) beats a 3-year streak at the same yield, every time.

Dividend growth — Does the payout rise every year? Even a tiny 2% raise keeps pace with inflation; a flat dividend loses 25% of its real purchasing power over a decade.

Risk-adjusted yield — A 12% yield paid by a leveraged mortgage REIT is not the same as a 5.6% yield paid by a 30-year aristocrat. We discount high yields that have never survived a recession.

Monthly REITs

1. Realty Income (O)
~5.6% yield
Streak: 30 yrs Div CAGR: ~4% Market cap: $45B+ Properties: 15,000+

The literal definition of a monthly dividend aristocrat. O has paid 650+ consecutive monthly dividends and raised them 30 years running. Its portfolio spans retail (7-Eleven, Dollar General), industrial, and gaming properties, with top-10 tenant concentration under 20%.

The catch: Growth is slow (~4% dividend CAGR). This is the anchor of a portfolio, not the engine.
2. STAG Industrial (STAG)
~4.4% yield
Streak: 13 yrs Div CAGR: ~1% Focus: US industrial warehouses

A pure-play bet on American e-commerce logistics. STAG owns ~570 industrial properties and collects rent from Amazon, XPO, FedEx, and hundreds of smaller 3PLs. Monthly distributions with 13 consecutive years of raises.

The catch: Single-sector concentration. A recession that hits industrial demand hits STAG harder than diversified O.

3. EPR Properties (EPR)
~7.3% yield
Streak: Reset 2021 Div CAGR: recovering Focus: Experiential real estate

Owns movie theaters, ski resorts, TopGolf locations, and amusement parks. Cut its dividend during COVID but has been raising again since 2021. High yield compensates for sector risk.

The catch: The 2020 cut is still fresh. Only include if you specifically want experiential exposure.

Monthly BDCs

4. Main Street Capital (MAIN)
~5.9% + specials
Streak: Never cut Pays: Monthly + supplementals Focus: Lower-middle-market BDC

The gold-standard business development company. MAIN has never cut its monthly dividend since going public in 2007 and pays semi-annual supplemental dividends on top. Invests in debt and equity of lower-middle-market US companies.

The catch: BDC income is taxed as ordinary income (non-qualified). Best held inside a Roth or IRA.
5. Prospect Capital (PSEC)
~11% yield
Streak: Cut in 2017 Pays: Monthly NAV trend: Eroding

Eye-catching double-digit yield. But PSEC has cut its dividend multiple times and its net asset value has drifted lower for years.

The catch: Included for completeness, not as a recommendation. High yield usually signals market-implied risk of a future cut.

Monthly Dividend ETFs

6. JPMorgan Equity Premium Income (JEPI)
~7.5% yield
Launched: 2020 Expense: 0.35% Strategy: Covered calls on S&P 500

Sells covered calls against a diversified basket of large-cap US stocks. The call premiums generate monthly income. Massively popular with near-retirees — over $35B in assets.

The catch: Distribution is ordinary income, not qualified. Capped upside. In a bull market, you underperform the S&P.

7. Amplify CWP Enhanced Dividend Income (DIVO)
~4.5% yield
Expense: 0.56% Strategy: Quality dividend + selective calls Holdings: ~25 names

A blend: holds 25 high-quality dividend growers (Apple, JNJ, Verizon, etc.) and writes covered calls opportunistically — not on the entire basket like JEPI. Monthly income, more capital appreciation than JEPI.

The catch: Higher expense ratio (0.56%) and smaller AUM than JEPI.
8. Invesco S&P 500 High Dividend Low Volatility (SPHD)
~3.9% yield
Expense: 0.30% Strategy: High-yield + low-vol screen Holdings: 50 names

Picks the 50 highest-yielding, lowest-volatility S&P 500 stocks. Pays monthly. Historically holds up better in drawdowns than the broader market.

The catch: Value-tilted portfolio can underperform during tech-led bull markets.
9. iShares Preferred & Income Securities (PFF)
~6.2% yield
Expense: 0.46% Strategy: Preferred stocks Holdings: 450+

Owns hundreds of preferred stock issues from banks, insurers, and utilities. Monthly distributions. Behaves more like a bond than equity — lower volatility, lower growth.

The catch: Interest-rate sensitive. Preferred prices fell 15%+ during the 2022 rate hike cycle.
10. iShares Core High Dividend (HDV)
~3.6% yield
Expense: 0.08% Strategy: 75 high-quality high-yielders Pays: Quarterly (honorable mention)

Included as an honorable mention — HDV technically pays quarterly, but many investors use it as a core-income position alongside their monthly payers. Cheap, high-quality, screens for financial strength.

Specialty & Higher-Risk Monthly Payers

11. AGNC Investment (AGNC)
~13% yield
Streak: Cut 2020, 2022 Type: Mortgage REIT Risk: High

Leveraged mortgage-backed securities portfolio. Pays double-digit monthly yield but has cut multiple times. Extreme interest rate sensitivity. Not a core holding.

The catch: AGNC's book value and dividend have both eroded over 10-year periods. The total return trails the dividend payouts.
12. Global X Nasdaq 100 Covered Call (QYLD)
~11% yield
Expense: 0.60% Strategy: Covered calls on Nasdaq-100 NAV trend: Declining

Sells at-the-money covered calls against the Nasdaq-100. Generates huge premium income (paid monthly), but the underlying NAV has drifted lower over time because all the upside gets called away.

The catch: Income comes at the cost of principal erosion. Over 10 years, QYLD has massively underperformed QQQ on total return.

Frequently asked questions

What's the highest-yielding safe monthly dividend stock?

"Safe" is relative. Realty Income at ~5.6% with a 30-year streak is about as conservative as monthly dividend stocks get. Yields meaningfully above that usually carry sector concentration, leverage, or covered-call NAV erosion risk.

Should I reinvest monthly dividends (DRIP) or take the cash?

If you're still accumulating, DRIP. Monthly reinvestment compounds faster than quarterly because you're buying more shares 12 times a year. If you're living off the dividends, take the cash and let the underlying shares keep compounding through appreciation.

Are monthly dividend stocks good for a Roth IRA?

Excellent — especially the REITs and BDCs on this list, since they pay non-qualified dividends taxed as ordinary income in a taxable account. Sheltering them inside a Roth eliminates the tax penalty entirely.

Can I just hold JEPI and call it done?

You could, but you'd be betting your entire income on one strategy. A diversified basket of 4–6 monthly payers (mixing REITs, BDCs, and covered-call ETFs) reduces single-strategy risk significantly without complicating your portfolio.
