Path to FIRE: A Dividend Calculator That Actually Works

Updated April 23, 2026 · Written by the YieldOS team

The standard FIRE calculator — multiply your annual expenses by 25, withdraw 4% a year, hope the market cooperates — works fine for total-return investors. For dividend investors it's the wrong framework. You don't care about portfolio value. You care about whether income covers expenses, reliably, without selling shares. That's a different math problem, and this guide walks through it.

We'll build the formula step by step, run three realistic examples, show where the 4% rule breaks down for income investors, and point to tools that automate the projection.

Why the 4% rule is the wrong question for dividend investors

The 4% rule came from the Trinity Study — a retrospective simulation of 30-year retirements using a 60/40 stock/bond mix, withdrawing 4% of the starting portfolio value adjusted for inflation. It works, broadly. But it assumes:

  • You'll sell shares every year to fund expenses. For income investors, that's the thing you built a dividend portfolio to avoid.
  • Sequence-of-returns risk is your main problem. It is — if you're selling. If you're living off dividends, sequence risk becomes dividend-cut risk, which is a different failure mode.
  • 25x expenses is your FIRE number. A 3% yielding portfolio actually needs ~33x. A 5% yielder only needs 20x. The multiple flexes with yield.
The dividend FIRE question is simpler: when does my annual dividend income, after tax, cover my annual expenses — without touching principal? Answer that and you have your exact year.

The core formula

Here's the full formula, broken into pieces:

# Your annual income in year N, with reinvestment
annualIncome(N) = P₀ × (1 + r) ^ N × y

# Where:
P₀ = current portfolio value
r = dividend reinvestment + dividend growth rate (annual)
y = current portfolio yield
N = years from today

# FIRE year = smallest N such that:
annualIncome(N) × (1 - taxRate) ≥ expenses × (1 + inflation) ^ N

In plain English: grow the portfolio each year by (reinvestment + organic dividend growth), multiply by the yield to get that year's income, subtract taxes, and compare against your expenses — which are themselves inflating each year. The first year income beats inflated expenses is your FIRE year.

The three numbers that actually matter

Three inputs dominate the answer. Everything else is noise at the precision most investors can forecast with.

1. Annual contribution

This is the biggest lever, especially early. Adding $500/month to a $50k portfolio compounds into a totally different outcome than adding $200/month. If you want to shorten FIRE by 5+ years, increase contributions. No other input moves the needle that much.

2. Portfolio yield

Not the SEC yield on one holding — your blended average. A 3% yield takes longer than a 5% yield to hit the same income number, but 5% yields often come from riskier holdings that cut in recessions. The sweet spot for sustainable FIRE is typically 3.5-4.5% blended yield from quality dividend growers, not 7-8% from covered-call ETFs.

3. Dividend growth rate

A company that grows its dividend 8%/year doubles your income in ~9 years. One that grows at 3%/year takes 24 years. For dividend growth investors, this is the whole ballgame. Look at 10-year dividend CAGRs, not one-year bumps — one year of special dividends can make the math look unrealistically good.

Three worked examples

Example 1: The early-career investor

Starting portfolio$15,000
Annual contribution$6,000
Blended yield4.0%
Dividend growth rate7.5%
Target annual expenses$45,000
Tax rate on dividends15%
Assumed inflation2.5%
Years to FIRE~28 years

That's long. But the same person upping contributions to $12,000/year cuts it to ~22. Upping to $18,000/year cuts it to ~18. Contribution size dominates everything.

Example 2: The mid-career investor

Starting portfolio$180,000
Annual contribution$24,000
Blended yield3.8%
Dividend growth rate6.0%
Target annual expenses$65,000
Tax rate on dividends15%
Assumed inflation2.5%
Years to FIRE~14 years

Mid-career investors benefit from compounding that's already happening. $180k generating $6,800/year in dividends is a real tailwind — that's another $6,800 reinvested each year on top of contributions.

Example 3: The coast-FIRE candidate

Starting portfolio$420,000
Annual contribution$0 (stops contributing)
Blended yield3.5%
Dividend growth rate7.0%
Target annual expenses$55,000
Tax rate on dividends15%
Assumed inflation2.5%
Years to FIRE~17 years

This is the interesting one. If you can hit a threshold and then just let compounding do its thing — contribute nothing, just reinvest dividends — you can still retire. The threshold depends on your timeline, but for a 45-year-old wanting to retire at 62, $400-500k in quality dividend growers is often enough.

Where the math lies to you

Dividend cuts aren't modeled

The formula assumes every company you own grows its dividend forever. In reality, 10-15% of dividend payers cut during a major recession (2008, 2020). A conservative model should stress-test a 20% income haircut in year N and see if you still survive. If a single recession blows up your FIRE plan, you're too concentrated or too levered on yield.

Inflation isn't 2.5%

It wasn't in 2022-2023. Lifestyle inflation is worse than CPI — your expenses grow faster than the basket Bureau of Labor Statistics tracks. Budget a 3-4% expense growth rate for planning. Being pleasantly surprised beats missing a target.

Taxes aren't flat 15%

Qualified dividends in the US are 0/15/20% depending on income. If you're retiring into a low-income year, your effective tax rate on dividends can drop to 0%. REITs pay non-qualified dividends taxed at ordinary rates. Mix matters.

Rule of thumb: model with conservative numbers (lower yield, lower growth, higher inflation, higher taxes) and your actual outcome is usually 2-4 years earlier than the projection. Model optimistically and you'll keep getting disappointed.

The honest bottom line

FIRE through dividends is slower than FIRE through index-and-sell. You'll typically hit a "coverage" number 2-4 years later than a pure total-return investor with the same contributions. What you trade those years for is a different retirement: one where you never sell into a crash, never worry about sequence risk, and where the income stream is visible in dollars-per-month instead of percentage-of-balance.

Whether that trade is worth it is a question only you can answer. But you can't answer it without running the numbers for your actual portfolio — so run them.
