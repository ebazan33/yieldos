The methodology difference matters more than the yield

VYM selects the top 50% of US stocks by forward dividend yield, weighted by market cap. That gives you a broad basket (450+ names) tilted toward financials, healthcare, and consumer staples — but also includes companies whose yields are high because their share price just collapsed.

DGRO screens for companies that have raised dividends for at least 5 consecutive years with a payout ratio below 75%. That quality filter excludes yield-traps but also excludes high-yielders that haven't been growing. The tradeoff: lower starting yield, faster compounding.

The 15-year yield-on-cost

At historical growth rates, $10,000 invested in DGRO today would generate ~$960/yr in dividends by year 15. The same $10,000 in VYM would generate ~$720/yr. DGRO's starting yield of 2.3% grows into 9.6% yield-on-cost — surpassing VYM by year 9.

Who should pick VYM

Pick VYM if you need higher cash flow now, you prefer Vanguard's fund structure (slightly better tax efficiency via the ETF/mutual-fund share class structure), or you want the absolute cheapest fee at 0.06%.

Who should pick DGRO

Pick DGRO if you're 10+ years from needing the income, you want a quality tilt (the 5-year growth screen has kept DGRO out of most dividend cuts), or you value a slightly more concentrated portfolio with companies like Microsoft, Apple, JPMorgan, and Johnson & Johnson driving growth.

Frequently asked

How much overlap is there between VYM and DGRO?

About 45–55% of holdings overlap by weight. Holding both gives a small diversification benefit but mostly doubles your exposure to the large-cap dividend payers both indexes pick.

Which has better total return?

DGRO has outperformed VYM on total return over the past 5 and 10 years because the growth screen captures stronger-compounding businesses. Past performance doesn't predict future results.

Are VYM dividends qualified?

Yes. Both VYM and DGRO pay primarily qualified dividends, which are taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% in the US) rather than ordinary income rates.

Is there a Canadian equivalent?

For Canadian investors, XDV (iShares Canadian Select Dividend Index ETF) is a rough equivalent to VYM for Canadian-listed dividend payers. There's no perfect Canadian DGRO clone.
